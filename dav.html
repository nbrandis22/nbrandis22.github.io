Digital Audio Visualizer

During my second year at UCLA, I decided to participate in another IEEE project called Digital Audio Visualizer (DAV). Throughout the year, we learned the basics of SystemVerilog and how to program a DE-10 Max Lite FPGA. Some of the projects we did included a calculator (using switches and 7-segment display), a piano (using switches and a buzzer), and Flappy Bird (using buttons and a VGA).

The big project at the end of the year was the Digital Audio Visualizer itself. The goal was to design three modules: a digital mic translator to buffer data detected from the digital mic, a FFT module to convert discrete time data to discrete frequency data, and a VGA module to display bars for the frequency data.

We chose to design a 32-point FFT, meaning we took 32 points in the time domain (sampled from the digital mic) and converted them into 32 points in the frequency domain. To do this, we implemented an FFT, where points are multiplied by each other and complex "twiddle factors" in five stages, to approximate integrating the time domain signal multiplied by a complex exponential across all time. We also designed the butterfly module to compute the outputs for a given butterfly unit given the inputs and the twiddle factor.

The VGA module was relatively simple. Essentially, we multiplied the positive outputs of the FFT by a scaling factor (a higher FFT output should result in a higher bar on the VGA, but must be scaled) and clocked those values in registers. The next VGA clock cycle, the VGA would color a specific pixel blue if the y-coordinate for the current bin was within the previously computed value stored in a register. This would result in 32 bins, which is what you can see in the video below.

The DAV shows frequency output, where lower measured frequencies are on the edges of the display, and higher frequencies are in the middle. Our DAV was voted "Best DAV" by IEEE members.
