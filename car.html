Remote Control Car

IEEE has a year-long extracurricular course intended for freshmen called "Open Project Space". When I did it, the final project was to create a car that can drive in "autopilot" mode, in which it can move approximately parallel to a wall, turning when necessary. This meant using an ultrasound sensor on one side of the car to measure distance from the wall, and an IR receiver/LED pair on the front of the car to detect when something is in front of the car.

On the software side, this meant implementing a PID controller using the values read by the ultrasound device. A PID controller is a mechanism where the error, derivative error, and integral error are continuously calculated and multiplied by different constants to get a value that affects the speed of the motors and thus the speed/direction of the car.

For example, a high error value (error value here means difference between the measured distance and the target distance) would result in the car changing direction by a large amount, whereas a low error value would result in the car barely changing its path. A high positive derivative error means the error is increasing rapidly, so the car should move the other way. The integral error measures the accumulation of error over time, and eliminates the steady-state error that remains from the other two terms.

​In addition to the "autopilot" mode, I created an Android app to control the car's movement over Bluetooth. The app has a joystick that allows you to remotely control the car's movement and an autopilot switch to allow the car to go into autopilot mode.
